Endometrial Microbiome — Clinical Context Endometrial tissue is considered near-sterile under normal conditions. A Lactobacillus-dominant endometrium (LEDA: ≥90% Lactobacillus) is strongly associated with higher implantation rates and clinical pregnancy success in ART. A non-LEDA profile (<90% Lactobacillus) correlates with reduced IVF/IUI success, recurrent implantation failure (RIF), and recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL). Any pathogen detected in endometrial tissue carries greater clinical significance than in vaginal samples.

Summary Conclusion

Across all 9 endometrial patient samples, the Custom Vaginal Database consistently outperformed the standard EMU database. The core issue is that the EMU general database was built for broad metagenomic applications and lacks sufficient representation of the specialized endometrial/vaginal microbiome. When reads cannot be mapped to known organisms, EMU forces them onto the nearest neighbor in its reference — which in these samples frequently produces freshwater, soil, and plant-associated organisms (Hydrotalea flava, Fimbriimonas ginsengisoli, Ralstonia solanacearum, etc.) that have zero relevance to human endometrial tissue.

Critically, none of the 9 patient samples met the LEDA threshold (≥90% Lactobacillus), which has direct implications for ART outcomes. Two samples (Drishya, Mrigakshi) showed pathogen-dominant, Lactobacillus-absent endometria — a finding that would have been completely missed under standard EMU reporting.

  • Use the Custom Vaginal Database exclusively for all endometrial and vaginal clinical reporting
  • All flagged pathogens (K. pneumoniae, Treponema, Brucella, N. meningitidis) require confirmatory testing before clinical action
  • Non-LEDA endometrial profiles should prompt clinical discussion about delaying embryo transfer
  • Retain the EMU general database only for research comparisons — never for primary clinical reporting
